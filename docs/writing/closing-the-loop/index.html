Closing the Loop: Fault Injection as a Ground-Truth Engine for a Dependency-Hardness Classifier

How a dependency-intelligence platform stopped guessing which service dependencies were load-bearing and started measuring it - by turning controlled fault-injection experiments into verified labels that continuously evaluate and tune an LLM-based hard/soft classifier.

Prabhav Nalhe · 2026 · ~6 min read

The problem: a classifier that was confident but unverifiable

At Meta, dependency-related incidents were roughly 15% of all company-wide top-severity events, and there was no single source of truth for what depended on what. A platform I worked on surfaced the dependency graph and then asked the question that actually matters for reliability: for a given dependency, is it hard or soft? A hard dependency takes the caller down with it. A soft one degrades gracefully - a cache miss, a fallback path, a feature that quietly turns off. You protect the hard ones and you spend nothing fortifying the soft ones. Get the call wrong in either direction and you either burn engineering on phantom risk or you leave a real single-point-of-failure exposed.

The classifier itself is LLM-based. It reads call sites, the introducing commit, service metadata, and the surrounding code, and emits a label plus a failure-impact summary and the SLO or SLI it expects to degrade. The hard part was never getting a model to produce a confident label. The hard part was knowing whether the label was correct. A model can read a code path and reason its way to hard with total fluency and still be wrong, because real production behavior depends on retry policy, timeouts, circuit breakers, and fallbacks that are not always legible from the source. Self-reported model confidence is not ground truth. So the central design question became: where does verified truth come from, and how do you get it at a scale that keeps a fleet-wide classifier honest over time?

The mechanism: disable one dependency, watch what breaks, record the label

The answer was to stop inferring criticality and start measuring it directly, by breaking things on purpose in a controlled environment. My team owns a chaos and fault-injection framework - a chaos-monkey-style system that injects controlled faults, such as severing a network path or blocking a dependency, against running services in non-production environments. I am a contributor to it, not its owner; my contributions were things like deprecating obsolete server APIs, fixing scheduler task-allocation failures, and adding a dependency-fault injection type. Across those contributions, the injection success rate reached about 97%. What I built on top of that framework was the experiment harness that turns it into a labeling engine.

The harness runs one clean experiment at a time. It takes a single dependency edge, injects a fault that disables exactly that edge in a controlled, non-production lab environment, and holds everything else constant. Then it observes: which SLOs and SLIs move, by how much, and whether the caller sheds the dependency cleanly or falls over. If disabling the edge breaches the caller's error budget or drops a key SLI, that edge is hard, and the experiment records it as a ground-truth label - not a model opinion, an observed outcome. If the caller absorbs the loss within budget, the edge is soft. The one-dependency-at-a-time isolation is what makes each result attributable: when only one thing changed, the degradation you measure can only be caused by that thing. The harness runs at a steady clip of roughly 700 experiments a day, and over the program the share of Tier-0/1 dependencies with experimental coverage went from 38% to 83%.

The loop: experiments become labels, labels keep the classifier honest

Here is where it compounds. Every experiment emits a row that says: this edge, under this injected fault, produced this SLO/SLI degradation, therefore this observed hardness. Those rows are exactly the verified ground truth the LLM classifier was missing. They become the experiment-verified label set the classifier is cross-validated and tuned against, so the model's prediction for an edge can be checked against what the experiment observed when that edge was severed in the lab.

The relationship between the two is directional and complementary. The classifier covers the whole fleet cheaply because inference is cheap; the fault-injection harness covers a smaller slice expensively but with certainty. So you let the cheap thing predict everywhere and the expensive thing verify and correct. When the harness disagrees with the model - the model said soft, the experiment breached the caller's error budget - that disagreement is the highest-value signal in the system. It points straight at the cases where the model's reasoning from code does not match production reality, often because of an undocumented retry storm, a missing fallback, or a shared resource the code did not make obvious. Those disagreements are triaged and fed back as corrected reference labels and adjustments to the classifier's prompting and decision thresholds, re-tightening the model against verified outcomes - no model weights are trained; the classifier is LLM-based and improved through eval and tuning, not gradient updates. Critically, a held-out slice of experiment-verified labels is never used for tuning and is reserved purely to score the model, which keeps the loop and the grading honest at the same time. The experiment harness is not just reliability tooling; it is the ground-truth source that keeps the classifier accurate as the fleet changes underneath it. The shipped classifier reached about 98% accuracy, and because that figure is measured against verified-experiment outcomes rather than the model's own confidence, the number is defensible rather than self-graded.

The evaluation methodology: code-verified truth, cross-validation, and anti-contamination

An accuracy number is only worth as much as the methodology behind it. The eval was designed to test the model against truth it could not see in advance, not to flatter it. Three principles held it together.

First, code-verified ground truth. The reference labels are not human guesses about what looks risky. They are anchored to verifiable artifacts - the actual call sites, the introducing commit, and the concrete failure-impact behavior - so a label can be traced to evidence rather than vibes. Second, fault-injection cross-validation. The classifier's predictions are checked against the independent, experimentally-observed outcomes from the harness. The two pipelines derive truth differently: one reasons over code, the other breaks the dependency in the lab and watches the SLO. When two independent methods agree, you trust the label; when they disagree, you have found either a model error or a gap in coverage, and both are useful. Third, anti-contamination. Because this is an LLM classifier and not a trained model, the contamination risk is in-context, not in a training set. The verified experiment outcome must not be visible to the model at inference time - not in the prompt, not in the tool results, not as a few-shot example - or you measure look-up instead of reasoning. The experiment-derived labels are held out as an independent grading set, so the classifier earns its score reasoning from code and tool calls alone, never from the answer.

Two smaller decisions mattered for trustworthiness. Lowering the model's sampling temperature, roughly 1.0 down to 0.2, reduced hallucinated code paths and made runs reproducible, which is essential when you are grading a model against a fixed label set and need the same input to give the same output. It is a reproducibility lever, not a hallucination cure. And the LLM component is not a retrieval-with-embeddings system; it is a tool-using agent harness that does structured retrieval by calling a set of purpose-built tools, on the order of a dozen-plus - build-graph queries, source fetches, blame and diff attribution, traces, service metadata - and reasoning over what they return. There are no vector embeddings and no semantic search anywhere in the path; retrieval is explicit, auditable tool calls, which is part of why a label can always be traced back to its evidence.

Why this is the right shape for a self-improving platform

The general lesson outlasts the specific system. If you have an LLM making a judgment at scale and an expensive way to observe ground truth at small scale, do not try to make the model self-certify and do not try to manually label everything. Build the cheap predictor and the expensive verifier as separate pipelines that derive truth by different means, then wire the verifier's disagreements back into the predictor. The disagreements are the highest-value signal in the system. They are where the model is wrong in ways its own confidence can never reveal, and they are finite - you only need to verify enough to keep the boundary calibrated, not the whole fleet.

The payoff is that accuracy stops being a one-time launch metric and becomes a maintained property. Services change, dependencies are added, fallbacks rot. A classifier graded once and trusted forever silently decays. A classifier with a continuous fault-injection loop behind it gets corrected by reality on a daily cadence, so its labels stay good as the system underneath it moves. And accuracy here is not academic: those hard/soft labels are what make the downstream dependency notifications worth subscribing to - the notifications the platform drove across 1,200+ services - and they powered a focused, multi-engineer risk-mitigation program I led that eliminated 50 risky Tier-0/1 dependencies in the monetization org, protecting roughly $100M of annual revenue exposure. A continuous ground-truth loop is what keeps those labels trustworthy as the fleet changes, and it is the reason the platform keeps improving instead of plateauing.

Takeaways

  • Do not let an LLM self-certify a judgment that has an observable ground truth. Build a cheap predictor (the fleet-wide classifier) and an expensive verifier (fault injection), and treat their disagreements as the highest-value signal.
  • Isolation is what makes fault injection a labeling engine: disable exactly one dependency, hold everything else constant, and the measured SLO/SLI degradation is causally attributable to that edge - a real label, not an opinion.
  • An accuracy number is only as good as its eval. Code-verified ground truth, cross-validation against independently-observed lab outcomes, and anti-contamination (experiment labels held out from the prompt, tools, and few-shot examples) are what turn 98% from a self-graded claim into a defensible one.
  • Lowering sampling temperature (roughly 1.0 to 0.2) reduced hallucinated code paths and made runs reproducible - a reproducibility lever, not a hallucination cure.
  • Continuous ground truth turns model accuracy from a launch-day snapshot into a maintained property: the loop corrects for drift through eval and tuning as services, dependencies, and fallbacks change - no model weights are trained.
